THE WINDOW

October in Caelum, Maine, had a particular smell. It was the smell of fulfilled promises and an inevitable end. It smelled of wet oak leaves rotting sweetly on the asphalt of the quiet streets, the smoke of chimneys beginning their annual work against the creeping cold from the north, and if you took a deep breath near the Penobscot River that bisected the town, you could smell cold water and river rocks. It was a homey smell. For most, at least.

The year was 2009. The great recession that was strangling the rest of the country seemed like just a distant news story on cable TV, something that happened to other people in other cities. Caelum wasn't rich, far from it, but she was stable. The paper mill was still operating, with its three punctual shifts, and the stores on Main Street, although some had an extra layer of dust on their windows, remained open.

People shopped at Caelum Hardware, ate breakfast at The Blue Jay Diner, and rented movies (DVDs' last gasps before streaming devoured everything) at the Blockbuster that stubbornly refused to die in the corner of the square. Life had a rhythm, a simple and predictable melody. A lullaby.

In the neighborhood known as Night Stars, named not for some spectacular celestial vision but for a developer with more poetry than common sense in the 1970s, the houses were mostly the same. Two-story ranch or colonial models with manicured lawns and white picket fences that needed a fresh coat of paint every couple of summers. It was the kind of place where kids rode their bikes until the sun went down and the biggest crime of the year was the theft of a garden gnome.

At 112 Maple Drive, a two-story colonial with faded blue shutters, the lullaby had long since stopped. In its place, a dense and heavy silence reigned, a silence that had teeth.

Sarah Blackwell felt this silence as soon as she opened her eyes at 6:15 in the morning. He was a constant companion, more present than her own husband sleeping beside her. The sound of Tom's gentle snoring was a welcome interruption, proof that not everything in his life had gone mute.

She stood up, her bare feet meeting the cold wooden floor, and pulled on the threadbare flannel robe that hung on the back of the door. The routine was an anchor. Sarah clung to her with the strength of a shipwrecked man clinging to a piece of wood.
